CHALLENGE

Design a native app of my choice for both iOS and Android.

PROBLEM

There are numerous apps - such as eBay - to sell personal belongings no longer used. On the other hand, the number of apps to give these items for free is close to none. I came across two, Trash Nothing and Find It, only relevant in the US and a possibility to put a price on the items, which defeats the purpose entirely.

SOLUTION

Create an app for users who wish to give away their belongings and for those who need them. It will also connect users with local charities if they wish to donate their items or money. The app has to be native as it uses push notifications and geolocation to facilitate the users’ needs and goals.

You can view the full iOS and Android prototypes.

PROCESS

I was in charge of both UX and UI processes. The design thinking approach guaranteed user-centric solutions as I was going through all phases of the project.

Let’s dive into some of the app’s deliverables and challenges:

USER FLOW

To define the MVP of my product, I created three simple user flows:

→  Post or request an item
→  Search items and contact owners
→  Look for a local charity and contact it

After user testing, I added the extra flow of making a direct donation to charity.

WIREFRAMES & UI PATTERNS

While working on low to high-fidelity wireframes, I quickly understood that familiar e-commerce UI patterns combined with a minimal and intuitive design were to be chosen over thinking too much outside the box.

Following iOS and Android respective design guidelines, users have access to different navigation elements:

↑ iOS : account icon + bottom navigation

Android : side entrance menu + top and bottom navigation + tab bar ↓

NATIVE REQUIREMENTS

iOS Human Interface and Material Design guidelines helped me meet the native requirements. Nevertheless, it was important to search for design solutions, which could give the app a strong identity without contradicting native guidelines and components.

Respective native elements: search bars, sliders, and alerts. ↓

TESTING & ITERATIONS

During usability tests, both iOS and Android users felt the designs were familiar and appropriate. The minimal and intuitive layout allowed them not to lose focus on their main goals.

Yet, the initial color palette did not win their heart: the contrast between the colors was too distracting. The dropdown menus were not a hit either.

After a few iterations, I decided on a warm and vintage palette and changed the menus into buttons. The app gained visual strength and cohesiveness.

« Contacting charities is great but I should also be able to give them a donation directly. »

I thought it was brilliant feedback and immediately added a primary call-to-action button to the screen.

UI STYLE GUIDE

After user testing (and a few extra iterations!), I perfected my style guide and finalized my designs.
The app was ready for launch!

RETROSPECTIVE

The insights I got throughout the process are numerous. For example, being a perfectionist while rapid prototyping or when working on low-fidelity wireframes is beside the point: these are not mockups! Early stages are meant to try out ideas and make mistakes. That being said, my choices when prototyping proved to be relevant and intuitive: testing them was a great success. It showcases the importance of user research, gathering and analyzing data, keeping my focus on user-centric solutions.

But I did get lost from time-time: I was so obsessed with meeting native requirements that I settled for colors and a couple of design solutions that did not work. Usability and preference testings are crucial when hitting a roadblock. They allowed me to incorporate major improvements to the looks and feels of Bazaar. I’ve learned that working alone always leads to a dead-end: what seems obvious and logical to you isn’t necessarily to others. Asking for feedback at each step of the process is key!

I wanted to create an app with a strong identity, combining modern and vintage, kindness and generosity. I do think it shows and I am very proud of the end result.
