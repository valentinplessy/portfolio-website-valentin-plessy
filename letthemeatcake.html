CHALLENGE

Design a responsive web app for French desserts and pastries recipes.

PROBLEM

Finding all sorts of recipes on the internet is easy but such varied choices can be overwhelming. What if people look for French pastries? Will they be sure to find professionally curated and high-quality ones? Will walkthroughs be explained clearly? Will they be provided with efficient help when needed?

SOLUTION

Create a responsive web app that will attract users looking for sweet French recipes. They may want to challenge themselves, improve their skills or look for inspiration. Levels of difficulty and preparation length, filtered search, technical help, and a shopping list will help them execute recipes with ease and precision.

USER RESEARCH & MVP

Interviews helped me pinpoint the problems users usually face when interacting with a recipe website.

Creating user personas and defining the Jobs To Be Done for the MVP of my project led to a straightforward user flow. I made sure users could achieve their goals with just a few clicks (or taps).

A recurrent frustration was discussed during the interviews: the lack of help for difficult steps or technical vocabulary. I began to ideate on how tips could be easily accessible without being a distraction from the walkthrough.

↑
The video walkthrough will be placed at the top of the recipe but won't start automatically.

↑
Tips will be activated by clicking/tapping on hyperlinks, allowing the users to choose whether they need the help or not.

WIREFRAMES &
USABILITY TEST

With the « mobile-first » approach, I started rapid-prototyping, using the crazy 8s method.

The low-fidelity wireframes turned out to be very successful during usability tests: all users achieved the required tasks with great ease.

It allowed me to further experiment with mid-fidelity wireframes and move on to the exciting UI phase!

MOODBOARD

I browsed A LOT of recipe websites and found myself bored with how they all essentially looked the same. I wanted to do something fresh and fun but also very French (but non merci, no baguette nor French flag color palette!).

Curiously, it was the vision of an American filmmaker who inspired me! Sofia Coppola’s Marie-Antoinette came to my mind and it clicked instantly. Its pastel color scheme but also its post-punk/pop/anachronic approach was exactly what I needed. Let Them Eat Cake! would be pop and fun, modern but old school, with breaking angles, colourful shapes, and a strong pop-art mood.

UI ELEMENTS &
ICONOGRAPHY

Designing UI elements and iconography was for me the most exciting part! I knew they would play a key role in giving the web app a unique personality and make it stand out from other home cooking websites.

Testers enjoyed the elements’ fun aesthetics while remaining familiar and intuitive.

BUILDING A RESPONSIVE WEB APP

This being my first responsive design, making sure style and elements all stood out within each breakpoint was brain-racking. I went back to wireframes and worked on the various breakpoints to finally defeat the responsive beast! Let it eat cake!

RETROSPECTIVE

With the excessive amount of recipe products on the web, it was important to define why users would choose mine. The niche of French sweet cuisine would solve this. It would also raise the problem of difficulty and force me to find the best solutions, which would never distract users from their goals. I had to rethink the navigation process a couple of times and make sure all necessary material and actions were right there in plain sight. That being said, the UX part of the project ran smoothly and was fruitful.

The visual aspect of my product was the main roadblock: I spent hours on home-cooking websites, trying to recreate the same UIs, unsatisfied. My choices were obvious at best, lifeless at worst. I worried that if I did not follow trends, my project would not work. During a break, it all clicked when I heard Ceremony from New Order, featured on Marie Antoinette’s soundtrack. I would follow my intuition and make something different, colourful, and fun! This taught me to be more confident in my design choices, as long as they remain familiar to users.
